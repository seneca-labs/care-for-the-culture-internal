The ask

What they want

The client is sponsoring two Freddie parties and wants a kiosk activation inside them. Freddie parties are high-energy LGBTQ nightlife events with DJs, drag performers, and thousands of attendees. The crowd is primarily Black and Latino MSM.

The brief: make an interactive kiosk that shows people zip-code-level HIV prevention data in a way that feels personal, fun, and shareable. Don't kill the party. Don't put a pharma logo on anything.

Ghost platform. No pharma branding. Zip code data is the core. Bridge the testing-to-PrEP gap.

*MSM = men who have sex with men. A behavior, not an identity.

The data

What we can work with

The data comes from AIDSVu.org, broken down by county. For each area we have:

Prevalence

How many people are living with HIV. The full weight of the epidemic in that place.

New diagnoses

What happened this year. What's active right now.

PrEP coverage

Who's protected. How many people in that area are on PrEP.

The gap

The distance between who's getting tested and who follows through to PrEP. Where the system loses people.

Racial breakdown

Splits by race, sex, age, and transmission category. The disparities are stark.

Personal answers

Each participant's own prevention status. Collected privately, used for follow-up.

We can show someone from Brooklyn that 1,225 per 100K are living with HIV, that 542 per 100K are on PrEP, and that for every new diagnosis, 24.6 people are protected. We can show someone from the Bronx that same ratio is 6.3. We can make that difference visible.

Strategy

From fear to community

The old approach

Show people how many HIV cases are near them. Hope fear motivates action.

This is what the original "heat map on a kiosk" concept was doing. It reads as a CDC dashboard at a drag party. Nobody posts a heat map of HIV prevalence in their neighborhood.

The new approach

Show people how protected their community is, where the gap is, and how they fit into the picture.

Make prevention feel like something the community is doing together. The shareable output has zero health data on it. The actual data travels through private channels the next morning, when people can absorb it.

The data is not the destination. It's the context that makes the experience meaningful. In one concept, the data is the visual itself. In the other, the data is the door you walk through to get to the social experience. Both reframe from diagnosis to participation.

Two directions

A map and a feed.

Signals

A US map where each participant becomes a data node at their zip code. The node's visual properties encode their area's HIV data. Connection lines link people who interacted near each other. The map fills all night. Data-first — you see your numbers before you take your photo. The output is a photo card: your face inside your node, the map behind you, your data visible.

The Feed

A photo-driven social feed. Take a group photo, then solo shots. Enter your zip, see a data visualization, answer the prompts, get a personalized photo card on a live feed. Runs on every kiosk, every phone, and a big ambient screen. Social-first — the photo is the hook, the data is woven in.

One shows you where you are. The other shows you who you're with. Both take your photo. Both send the same morning text.

The system

What both concepts share

Zip code entry

Every user enters their zip. The data is localized to their county. This is the client's core requirement and it's non-negotiable in both concepts.

Prevention journey question

"Where are you on your prevention journey?" Four answers. No wrong ones. "I'm good" works for anyone who doesn't want to engage further.

Photo moment

Both concepts capture a photo. When it happens and what it means to the experience is where they diverge.

Morning-after text

11am the next day. Your photo from the night, then personalized data and PrEP/testing resources based on zip and answer. They're sober. They can actually read.

Ghost platform

No pharma name. No Freddie branding on the kiosk. "Care for the culture" is the only visible name, and it's subtle.

Data capture

Phone numbers, zip codes, answer distributions. The morning text click-through rate on PrEP/testing links is the conversion metric.

SIGNALS

The map you know. The data you don't.

Signals

How it works

The map starts as a field of black dots — the continental US rendered as a tight dot grid on white. Each dot is geography, nothing more. When you enter your zip, a data card surfaces: your area's prevalence, new diagnoses, PrEP rate, and the gap. You answer the prevention journey question. Your node drops at your county.

The moment your node lands, it starts transforming the dots around it. If you're on PrEP, your teal spreads the farthest — radiating outward through the surrounding dot field. That spread radius isn't decorative. It represents the statistical protective footprint of one covered person in their community. Smaller spread, smaller coverage. The gap between who's protected and who isn't is visible in how far each color actually reaches. The zip-level view shows it at block scale: dots sized by local prevalence, your color bleeding through the neighborhood dot by dot.

After your node lands, the kiosk takes your photo — composited into the node, a face inside the data. By 2am the map is 150+ nodes, each one a person, a place, and a coverage picture. Your output is a photo card: your face in your node, the colored map behind you, your area's numbers visible.

THE FEED

A live social platform with HIV data built into every interaction.

The Feed

Why a feed?

Everyone at a Freddie party is on their phone at some point. They're checking Grindr, scrolling Hinge, opening Instagram. The apps they're using treat health status as an optional field buried three screens deep. Grindr shows "PrEP: Yes" next to height and weight like it's the same kind of information. There's no context, no geographic data, no sense of what's happening in your zip code.

Meanwhile, nobody at a party wants to walk up to a health data kiosk. They want to take photos, find people, be seen.

Discretion

No dating-app profiles with "pos" or "neg" in plain text. Health info is collected privately, used for follow-up, not display.

Context

Your PrEP status on Grindr is decontextualized. The Feed shows your area's data alongside the social experience.

Faces

Many hookup apps are built around faceless browsing. The Feed is face-first. You showed up. Your face is on the card.

Follow-through

Apps collect health status but do nothing with it. The Feed uses your answers to send personalized resources the next morning.

The Feed

Three surfaces, one system

The kiosk

Entry point

Physical station at the party. This is where photos happen, data gets collected, cards get created. Multiple kiosks across the venue. Think of it as the ritual.

The phone

Extension

A QR code at the kiosk opens the same feed in your mobile browser. No app download. Browse from the dance floor, the afterparty, or the next morning. This is the utility.

The ambient display

Spectacle

Large screen near the DJ booth. Real-time aggregate. How many people joined, which zip codes, the room's collective PrEP rate. No individual data. The DJ can reference it. Data as artwork.

All three read from the same backend. A photo taken at kiosk 3 appears on every other kiosk, every phone, and the ambient display within seconds.

The Feed

The kiosk flow

1

Idle screen

The feed itself. A scrolling wall of photo cards from the night. Faces and energy. No health language. This is what pulls you over.

2

Group photo

Your crew walks up. Camera. 3-2-1 countdown. Retake if needed. Group shot becomes the cover card.

3

Solo photo

Each person takes a turn. Quick, clean. Your solo shot becomes your individual card.

4

Zip code

"Enter your zip code." The gateway to the data.

5

Data moment 8-10 sec

Your neighborhood's shape appears, isometric, glowing. Particles for prevalence. Color temperature for PrEP. Negative space for the gap. Numbers integrated: "1,225 per 100K" / "542 on PrEP" / "Gap: 24.6x." You can't skip it without seeing it.

6

Health prompts

Prevention journey question. Testing status. "Prefer not to share" is always the same size as every other option. 6-9 seconds total.

7

Vibe selection

"What's your energy tonight?" Pick a visual vibe. This determines your card's gradient. The fun, personal part.

8

Card generation

Your photo gets its treatment: gradient frame, grain texture, color palette. Group card blends everyone's palettes. Both enter the feed.

9

Share

QR for phone feed. Phone number for text. Print a polaroid. Opt in or out of appearing on the feed. Next person steps up.

Solo users: the group photo step is skipped. Their card enters the feed as a standalone. Solo people are first-class participants.

The Feed

Where the data lives

How each of the six data points surfaces in the experience.

Prevalence

Particle density in the data moment visualization. More particles, more people living with HIV. The number appears as typography inside the visual. 8-10 seconds.

New diagnoses

Rate of new particles arriving on screen. High new-Dx areas have a visible stream. Number alongside prevalence.

Testing

The "Do you know your status?" prompt makes the user self-reflect on their own testing in the context of their area's data.

PrEP coverage

Color temperature in the data moment. Warmer tones mean higher adoption. Also appears on the phone feed when filtering by area: stats ride alongside the people cards.

The gap

Visible negative space between particle clusters. The gap ratio number is called out. Also in the morning text: "X people get tested, only Y follow through to PrEP."

Prevention journey answer

Collected in the health prompts. Never displayed publicly. Drives morning text personalization and aggregate reporting.

The Feed

The kiosk idle screen

When nobody's using it, the kiosk shows the feed. Photo cards scrolling, drifting, breathing with subtle motion. Gradient auras shifting. A living wall of faces from the party.

Group cards are larger, solo cards cluster around them. Tap a group card to see the individuals. Counter updates in real-time: "247 people on The Feed tonight."

The aesthetic is bold. Deep magentas, electric oranges, purples. It should feel like a party flyer, not a dashboard. Every card has a unique gradient so the feed has visual variety.

The Feed

The party on your phone

Scan the QR at the kiosk or get a text link. The feed opens in your mobile browser. No app download.

This is where the second data touchpoint happens. When you filter the feed by area ("show me people from Brooklyn"), the area's HIV stats surface alongside the results. "Brooklyn: 1,225 per 100K living with HIV. 542 per 100K on PrEP." You're looking for faces. The data rides alongside.

Tap a card to see someone's full photo. If they linked Instagram, you can follow. The connection layer is lightweight. This is a party-specific tool for the night and the day after.

The feed stays live after the event. For future events, same platform, new feed. Someone who joined at NYC can browse the LA feed when it goes live. The user base accumulates.

People spend 20+ minutes browsing the phone feed. The data encounters happen every time they scroll past an area filter.

The feed becomes a record of who was there. Browse the next day, the next week. The connection doesn't end when the party does.

Know Thy Self × The Feed

Where the two concepts merge

Know Thy Self isn't a separate activation — it's a new entry point into The Feed. The quiz gives people a reason to stop. The Feed gives the output a place to live.

Quiz output → Feed card

Your polaroid photo enters The Feed tagged with your personality type. The type becomes the gradient treatment on your card. Every archetype has its own color — so the feed builds visual variety across the night.

Stat → data layer

The personalized stat shown during the quiz pulls from the same zip-code dataset powering Signals. When someone filters the phone feed by area, the same stat rides alongside. The data encounter happens twice — once during the quiz, once while browsing.

Morning text

The morning text personalizes to the archetype. "The Adventurer" gets a different message than "The Planner." Same PrEP and testing links — different framing. Higher click-through because it sounds like it's meant for them.

The net effect: Know Thy Self is an on-ramp for people who wouldn't approach a data kiosk. It lowers the barrier, raises the engagement, and feeds everything into the same backend. The Feed lives. The data accumulates. The platform grows.
